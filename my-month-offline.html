My Month Offline

personal record
2026-03-02 – 2026-XX-XX • status: in-progress

This page is a record of my digital declutter conducted from March 2nd to April 2nd 16th. My chronic use of the internet appears to have psychological effects such as low motivation, a grealty increased task initiation threshold causing procrastination, and disrupted sleep. As of March 2nd, I'm behind on my homework by weeks and have missed several tests. My bedtime ranges between 1AM and 4AM. This is unsustainable and greatly negatively impacts my life despite the value I gain from my online relationships and communities. Since I have met in-person many of the people I interact with online, I don't really see the internet as purely parasocial. I strongly reject the idea that discord and twitter friendships are not true socialization, but I do realize that using social media as much as I do confers more harm than good. I don't know where to draw the limit so in the spirit of Cal Newport's "digital declutter" and elimination diets, I will cut everything out for a month.

In addition to organizing my cluttered life, I want to use this as a time to catapult progress on my specific goal of moving to the Bay Area. AGI timelines are short. Moving to San Francisco is high-leverage, and I think I'd move much faster there in every respect that I want to. I don't know what I want to do with respect to the current timelines situation, but it's nigh impossible to figure that out in an irrelevant place.

My internet restriction started on March 2nd, 2026 and lasts until April 2nd 16th, 2026. On April 2nd 16th, I will start carefully reintroducing things to my life and seeing how they affect me.

I am recording each day of the declutter to see how eliminating the ambient digital distractions from my life improves. I've occasionally seen remarkable improvement with two-day long "digital fasts" but have lacked the discipline to enforce them for a longer period of time. I frequently slip-up and lose interest in bearing out the first few days of boredom. In order to combat that this time, I am making a $20:$0 bet with a friend on whether I can ride out the month (e.g. if I slip-up, I pay $20, but if I don't, they don't pay anything).

Restricted Activities and My Substitutes

Restrictions and Guiding Philosophy

During a digital declutter, Cal Newport advises creating a list of restricted activities and pairing that with a list of alternatives to intentionally use instead. In full honesty, I have trouble revisiting Cal Newport's work because I dislike his writing style and find his recent writing on AI to be borderline sophistic and occasionally even incorrect, but his ideas on technology use and the value of uninterrupted focus are often good and original regardless. Sometimes detailed elaborations on common sense ideas, like much of Newport's work is, can be tremendously valuable to aiding implementation. I owe many of my accomplishments to carefully following Newport's ideas.

The following is a list of things I'm going to be cutting out or severely restricting:

  • YouTube
    • Exception: Video Tutorials or Information
    • No use of algorithmic feed or subscription feed allowed
    • YouTube not allowed on phone
  • Reddit
    • Exception: For information. Must be Through Internet Archive.
    • No algorithmic feed, logging in on account or scrolling allowed.
  • Podcasts
  • Music
  • Twitter
  • Discord
    • Exception: Sunday (1:20PM to 1:35PM) for an ongoing meetup
    • Exception: Wednesday (6:30PM to 6:45PM) a club
    • During those exception times, Discord may not be used for anything other than checking meeting info and must be deleted/logged out immediately after
  • Online meetups
  • My phone
    • Softer rule due to logistics. Use flip phone or leave phone at home when possible.
    • Maps always allowed
  • Forums, news, blogs
    • No Hacker News
    • No news via algorithmic feed
    • No Substack
    • No reading blogs
    • No mindless browsing
    • Task-tangential rabbitholes
    • Exception: forums that show up in google results, e.g. StackOverflow or StackExchange (if actually necessary for a task)
    • Exception: literal newspaper
  • Caffeine
    • No coffee
    • No chai
  • TV
    • Exception: Watching a movie with friends or family
  • Communications
    • Texting allowed but avoid entire conversations. Only scheduling calls or exchanging important information or logistics.
    • Calls are allowed
  • Google & LLMs
    • LLMs not allowed on cell phone.
    • Only use LLMs for deep engagement when learning or getting help for something
    • Google for simple queries or necessary information allowed always
    • Google for idle curiosity not allowed
  • Above all: No use of stimulating distractions to quell negative emotions

Broadly, I aim to eliminate the internet as a space for recreation or ambiently filling silent or bored moments, which means even cutting out activities like aimlessly browsing Wikipedia or Googling things for fun, though it would be too much to cut out Wikipedia or Google as services.

The bet portion of this is on an honor system. I would consider myself to have failed if I turn off my website blocker and scroll Twitter for a while in intentional violation of my rules, or if I was to log onto Reddit or Discord or put on a Youtube video or podcast to fill a moment of uncomfortable boredom. Those things are all poison to me so I will be harsh with myself about the rules for those. I don't consider slipping up my softer rules to be grounds for failure. It's not really an issue if I google something out of curiousity or read a wikipedia page, but my rules about those serve as a reminder to be mindful on replacing that behavior with their deeper alternatives, like read a book on a topic instead of just the top section of the topic's wikipedia page. The same is true of my smart phone. It's really hard to avoid bringing my phone places, so it's not a fail if I take my phone to school, but I want to start leaving it at home more or taking my flip phone when I can.

More than those things, the main point of the bet is to avoid giving up entirely when something small happens or I'm facing some negative emotion I want to escape with online distractions.

For replacement activities, Newport reccomends aggressive exploration of offline or less-stimulating alternatives. I'm segmenting these activities into "Deep Solo Activities" and "Deep Social Activities".

Deep Solo Activities

It's important to directly replace things some of I use being online for with direct behaviors to do as alternatives.

  • In place of trying to fill silent moments with YouTube, podcasts, and music:
    • Reading books
    • Journaling
  • In place of the internet for information with Twitter, Reddit, and news:
    • Reading non-fiction books on anything that interests me
    • Working through a personal reading list of papers on topics I want to understand
    • Learning the tools I already use (like working through advanced Vim tutorials)
  • In place of using the internet as a distraction to quell my negative emotions:
    • Staring at a wall
    • Going for walks
    • Running outside
  • In place of my general internet-based procrastination:
    • Actually doing homework
    • The Project
  • In place of caffeine:
    • Herbal tea
    • Herbal tea with milk
    • Other non-caffeinated drinks, e.g. orange juice
  • In place of my phone:
    • Flip phone
    • Not taking my phone with me at all
    • Leaving my phone in a lockbox on my desk

Along with direct replacements to certain activities, not wasting so much time on the internet frees an enourmous amount of time. Even accounting for the time that will be spent working, I should have more than enough time to apply myself at things I've previously lacked the energy to do. I want to intentionally spend that surplus on certain things:

  • Climbing more often, while being intentional with how I spend each session, instead of going into the gym with no plan as I often do. From now, either a climbing session will be high volume to gain endurance and practice technique, or a session will be high intensity to gain strength and power while climbing at my limit.
  • Lifting more often, while limiting myself to six heavy sets between two strength-focused exercises and twelve to sixteen hypertrophy sets between four to five hypertrophy-focused exercsies.
  • Stretching: instead of spending an hour before bed on reddit, I will use that time to gain flexibility, a long-time goal of mine
  • Running: instead of time I spend doing literally nothing, I think I could fit in sessions to regain my cardio ability

Deep Social Activities

Aside from the informational stimulation I get from the internet, much of my socialization is online too. This is quite negative for me, because I find I have few friends in-person and struggle socially. In-person socialization is really good for my mood and being deprived of this has often had terrible consequences for me.

  • In place of the internet for (para-)socialization with Discord, Twitter, Forums, and podcasts:
    • Aggresively exploring in-person clubs on campus
    • Attending in-person meetups of any kind more often
    • Talking to new people at my climbing gym for an hour after my specific training sessions are done
    • Asking people if they'd want to get lunch with me
    • Going out with friends on the weekend
  • With the time extra time that being decluttered creates, I want to:
    • Go to more events and parties
    • Try something new in a social capacity every week
    • Organize things of my own

The Project

My AGI timelines have been on the shorter end as long as I can remember having them. Even in middle school, discovering Bostrom's Superintelligence with absolutely no idea or comprehension of what I was reading, I felt that the perspective predicting short-timelines and a fast-takeoff to be intuitively correct. It's strange that the Bostrom view was so familiar to me then, despite how normal it was then to dismiss it all as crazy. Now that years later everyone's updated to their conservative "AGI possible by 2030" estimates, my instinct is to expect progress to go faster. If everyone has a systemic bias towards AI progress going slow, and like clockwork they update towards faster progress models, it seems reasonable to start wondering whether estimates on the faster end are more calibrated.

Alas, sooner or later I have no doubt that it's happening. Over the last few years, I've started to absorb the import of what's coming. There have been times I've waxed into depression. I've tried to push myself to make the most of our remaining years of normality by optimizing my life for cheerful leisure in passive response to what's coming. There have been times I've tried to get to work. All those efforts – both at leisure and effort – have failed. It's hard to push through my overall bad feelings on how Things will end up Going and respond in a way I find principled (even giving up requires mental commitment). My constant use of the internet and cluttered mind have definitely harmed my attempts to make progress on something like this, and added to the emotional confusion I have on how to respond.

The clutter in my life comes from – I heavily suspect – an attempt to drown out negative emotion with noise. Many negative emotions can be dealt with. I've been cluttered in similar ways that I am now before and resolved it each time by addressing the cause. The main negative emotion underlying my clutter right now is my fear of short timelines, and motivating the declutter is an attempt to address that.

But it's hard to think through things so divorced from where they're really happening. I want to move to San Francisco in order to really process it. The main purpose of this declutter is to help me figure out how to get there in a semi-permanent capacity as soon as possible.

Though college is nice, I don't want to be here. It will take all that I'm capable of to get out of here and where I want to be, the place I've dreamed of going for years. To echo Shane Legg: The time has come now not for idle rumination, but for doing.

Not having the internet as a potential distraction to whittle away my morning on meant I made it to the 9:45 bus when I usually procrastinate for a few hours and take the 10:39 bus to school, all despite waking up slightly late today. I think I need about 9 hours of sleep a night given how intense my lifting + climbing is right now. I find deadlifting massively increases fatigue and I often need days to feel recovered.

I read some of Nick Bostrom's old book Superintelligence on the bus to school. I find it's much easier to read without the potential for a distraction, since it feels like the possibility of opening twitter always tugs on my attention. I also didn't listen to any music on the bus except for half a minute when I was trying to find a sound to play on my headphones to block out the rattling of the bus. I don't consider that a slip-up from the policy or using music for recreation though. Interestingly, that half a minute sounded really good. I'm reminded of the time that Michael Pollan (who might be a fraud or dumbass but that's irrelevant to this) said he quit coffee for a month and his first cup of coffee after his abstinence felt noticeably wonderful. The same might be true of music, if you stop listening to it constantly to fill silence. I don't think that the brain adapts to music the way it adapts to coffee. Taking caffeine every day would lead to adaptation fast, but listening to music daily probably wouldn't. The key is to have the sound feel novel. So maybe a practice like allowing most of your day to be silent, would neutralize the adaption – whereas you might have to drink coffee once a week or less to not adapt to it.

I don't benefit from most stimulants I take and dislike the psychological effect of almost all stimulants – caffeine being the only exception. Being adapted to caffeine, like I am now, has no benefit. I've decided to quit caffeine for this declutter month, and take it like once a week after that for recreation.

Along with music, catching the bus on time, and reading feeling easy – another instant change I've noticed from the declutter is that I eat so much faster. I usually wait until like 8pm to start eating protein to fill out my macros, but yesterday I came home and instantly made a protein shake and ate my ration of greek yogurt. That was quite surprising and a nice change. I've read from other accounts of declutters that people get similar nice behavioral changes that show up on week 2 as well. When not constantly distracted, it seems I need so much less will power to move through life. Despite what I thought, I have the executive function of an average 20-year old (or better), but it seems being distracted all the time makes me functionally a 10-year old.

Regarding work itself, though my situation with sleep and overall distractedness is improving, I did just spend like 25 minutes writing this instead of working – so I don't want keeping this log to be a distraction. I might have to come up with a system to prevent that.

I like Jocko Willink's philosophy on discipline but prior to the declutter felt I couldn't really click with it. If the effectiveness of your will power is fundamentally bottlenecked or restricted (as constant distraction does to it), then mentally forcing yourself to do something just doesn't work. It's like pressing the accelerator in a car that won't start. The point of discipline is to consciously push that accelerator – not to constantly get out of the car and be forced to manually push it, which would break anyone's resolve – but something about my exposure to the internet reliably disrupts the engine to the point I've had to shove the car everywhere I want it to go my whole life.

I think my ADHD-tendencies make me more vulnerable to harm from distractions. If you gave my psychology and low resistance to distraction to the average person, it would ruin their life. To everyone else, the ADHD makes me look like I'm just "lazy". The irony is if you swapped our positions I think I would just vastly exceed most people and most people would fair far worse than I do even when the ADHD is least controllable. At the risk of arrogance, I think what I'll get done over the next few weeks – and even after that – should do well to prove my point. Well, I guess it's just arrogance. I'm extremely angry at my years of constant failure and personal dissapointment with myself and I'm very much "out for blood" to prove my capability. I think that will be useful. In some respects I have a deeply violent undercurrent to my personality. I just don't accept not getting what I want. I think I see aspects of myself in the There Will Be Blood guy or Alex Honnold. The overlapping portions aren't the good or effective parts. It's a very dark part of myself. Most who share these aspects probably end up as heroine addicts and not successful entrepenuers or climbers. (Honnold's partial auto-biography "Alone on the Wall" paints a pretty different picture of him than the media coverage does and which Free Solo only lightly touches on, and it's what I'm referring to here.) But regardless, when used correctly this ego-centric desire to prove you're better than everyone can be effective fuel. It's just that effective fuel can drive you into a ditch just as fast as it can take you somewhere worth going. These traits are probably hereditary. It's important to have a stopping point to it, like Honnold appeared to have with El Cap and which the There Will Be Blood guy just entirely lacked in the movie. I think the only way to get this to stop is to have some kind of goal like Honnold did. I think it stems from some kind of deeply-ingrained feeling of inadequacy and self-resentment that starts as a child and just becomes core to your personality as you get older. All you have to do to get it to stop is free solo El Cap I guess.
