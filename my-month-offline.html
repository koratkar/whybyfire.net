My Month Offline

personal record
2026-03-02 – 2026-??-?? • status: in-progress

This page is a record of my digital declutter conducted from March 2nd to April 2nd. My chronic use of the internet appears to have psychological effects such as low motivation, a grealty increased task initiation threshold causing procrastination, and disrupted sleep. As of March 2nd, I'm behind on my homework by weeks and have missed several tests. My bedtime ranges between 1AM and 4AM. This is unsustainable and greatly negatively impacts my life despite the value I gain from my online relationships and communities. Since I have met in-person many of the people I interact with online, I don't really see the internet as purely parasocial. I strongly reject the idea that discord and twitter friendships are not true socialization, but I do realize that using social media as much as I do confers more harm than good. I don't know where to draw the limit so in the spirit of Cal Newport's "digital declutter" and elimination diets, I will cut everything out for a month.

My internet restriction started on March 2nd, 2026 and lasts until April 2nd, 2026. On April 2nd, I will start carefully reintroducing things to my life and seeing how they affect me.

I am recording each day of the declutter to see how eliminating the ambient digital distractions from my life improves. I've occasionally seen remarkable improvement with two-day long "digital fasts" but have lacked the discipline to enforce them for a longer period of time. I frequently slip-up and lose interest in bearing out the first few days of boredom. In order to combat that this time, I am making a $20:$0 bet with a friend on whether I can ride out the month (e.g. if I slip-up, I pay $20, but if I don't, they don't pay anything).

Restricted Activities and My Substitutes

During a digital declutter, Cal Newport advises creating a list of restricted activities and pairing that with a list of alternatives to intentionally use instead. In full honesty, I have trouble revisiting Cal Newport's work because I dislike his writing style and find his recent writing on AI to be borderline sophistic and occasionally even incorrect, but his ideas on technology use and the value of uninterrupted focus are often good and original regardless. Sometimes detailed elaborations on common sense ideas, like much of Newport's work is, can be tremendously valuable to aiding implementation. I owe many of my accomplishments to carefully following Newport's ideas.

The following is a list of things I'm going to be cutting out or severely restricting:

  • YouTube
  • Reddit
  • Twitter
  • Discord (except for 15 minutes on Sunday and Wednesday to check information for IRL meetups I attend)
  • Podcasts
  • Music
  • Online meetups
  • My phone
  • Hacker News, Substack
  • Caffeine (added March 3rd)

Since I sometimes need YouTube and Reddit when searching for things, I am only barring them as recreational activities. Using them to fill silence and using them intentionally to find information are completely separate in my experience. I am blocking reddit on all devices and only accessing it when necessary via the internet archive.

Since I need my phone for maps and communication sometimes, I'm going to leave it at home in its lockbox for a month, using a physical keyboard connected via USB to type if necessary. I am switching to an old flip phone I will carry around at school instead to take calls in urgent situations.

Broadly, I aim to eliminate the internet as a space for recreation or ambiently filling silent or bored moments, which means even cutting out activities like aimlessly browsing Wikipedia or Googling things for fun, though it would be too much to cut out Wikipedia or Google as services.

The following is a list of things I'm going to substitute them with:

  • Staring at a wall and going for walks in place of using the internet as a distraction to quell my negative emotions
  • Reading books in place of the internet to fill silent moments
  • Homework in place of obsessively checking the news to keep track of things
  • My different fitness hobbies, e.g. climbing, lifting, running in place of aimless online recreation
  • Applying for jobs in place of chatting on discord
  • Clubs (climbing, philosophy club) in place of socializing on discord
  • Meeting friends in place of socializing on discord or twitter
  • Flip phone in place of phone

Daily Log

March 2nd

5:15PM – Wrote my list of restrictions, told online friends I would be offline for accountability, logged out of all social media accounts, and made the $20 enforcement agreement with friend. My month offline has begun.

I still feel a little bit like social media is "accessible" through the internet because I haven't added youtube to my website blocker. I want to feel offline in a way I'm not sure this rule allows. I will see how I feel in a bit. Having so much access to the internet feels like leaving a million tabs open in my brain, and creating new restrictions is like force quitting the whole application. It might take some time to fully close those loops. Aside from that small gap in my rule set, I feel quite confident about this project overall given the harshness of the agreement I made and severity for failure.

March 3rd

10:44AM

Yesterday I went to bed at 10:30. It was a little later than I liked but I managed to read a book for 20 minutes prior to sleeping. Apparently Bryan Johnson is right about this, because my HRV was higher and resting heart rate was lower than usual. My resting heart rate is around 60BPM. In the summer my average was around 55BPM until I over-trained one week and it's since then hovered at 60BPM. I'm not sure what to do to lower it again. Maybe my cardio is undertrained? Maybe I'm just suffering a residual overtraining that hasn't abated despite deload weeks? I'm not sure. I'll start with trying to do 20 minutes of cardio every day with two of those sessions being HIIT.

Not having the internet as a potential distraction to whittle away my morning on meant I made it to the 9:45 bus when I usually procrastinate for a few hours and take the 10:39 bus to school, all despite waking up slightly late today. I did read a book before leaving for the bus too, meaning I could've taken an earlier bus but I stopped early enough to leave on time so I don't think it's worth changing the rules to combat this behavior. I think I need about 9 hours of sleep a night given how intense my lifting + climbing is right now. I find deadlifting massively increases fatigue and I often need days to feel recovered.

I read some of Nick Bostrom's old book Superintelligence on the bus to school. I find it's much easier to read without the potential for a distraction, since it feels like the possibility of opening twitter always tugs on my attention. I also didn't listen to any music on the bus except for half a minute when I was trying to find a sound to play on my headphones to block out the rattling of the bus. I don't consider that a slip-up from the policy or using music for recreation though. Interestingly, that half a minute sounded really good. I'm reminded of the time that Michael Pollan (who might be a fraud or dumbass but that's irrelevant to this) said he quit coffee for a month and his first cup of coffee after his abstinence felt noticeably wonderful. The same might be true of music, if you stop listening to it constantly to fill silence. I don't think that the brain adapts to music the way it adapts to coffee. Taking caffeine every day would lead to adaptation fast, but listening to music daily probably wouldn't. The key is to have the sound feel novel. So maybe a practice like allowing most of your day to be silent, would neutralize the adaption – whereas you might have to drink coffee once a week or less to not adapt to it.

I don't benefit from most stimulants I take and dislike the psychological effect of almost all stimulants – caffeine being the only exception. Being adapted to caffeine, like I am now, has no benefit. I've decided to quit caffeine for this declutter month, and take it like once a week after that for recreation.

Along with music, catching the bus on time, and reading feeling easy – another instant change I've noticed from the declutter is that I eat so much faster. I usually wait until like 8pm to start eating protein to fill out my macros, but yesterday I came home and instantly made a protein shake and ate my ration of greek yogurt. That was quite surprising and a nice change. I've read from other accounts of declutters that people get similar nice behavioral changes that show up on week 2 as well. When not constantly distracted, it seems I need so much less will power to move through life. Despite what I thought, I have the executive function of an average 20-year old (or better), but it seems being distracted all the time makes me functionally a 10-year old.

Regarding work itself, though my situation with sleep and overall distractedness is improving, I did just spend like 25 minutes writing this instead of working – so I don't want keeping this log to be a distraction. I might have to come up with a system to prevent that.

12:45PM Made it to an office hours meeting on time and finished one assignment ahead of deadline – both of which are quite rare for me. I still feel the "tug" of the internet on my attention, like the tabs being open in my mind that I mentioned yesterday. It's annoying. Either this will go away in a few days as my mind adapts, or I might have to make some other change. I'm not sure what it is.
