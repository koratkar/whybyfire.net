My Month Offline

personal record
2026-03-02 – 2026-??-?? • status: in-progress

This page is a record of my digital declutter conducted from March 2nd to April 2nd. My chronic use of the internet appears to have psychological effects such as low motivation, a grealty increased task initiation threshold causing procrastination, and disrupted sleep. As of March 2nd, I'm behind on my homework by weeks and have missed several tests. My bedtime ranges between 1AM and 4AM. This is unsustainable and greatly negatively impacts my life despite the value I gain from my online relationships and communities. Since I have met in-person many of the people I interact with online, I don't really see the internet as purely parasocial. I strongly reject the idea that discord and twitter friendships are not true socialization, but I do realize that using social media as much as I do confers more harm than good. I don't know where to draw the limit so in the spirit of Cal Newport's "digital declutter" and elimination diets, I will cut everything out for a month.

My internet restriction started on March 2nd, 2026 and lasts until April 2nd 16th, 2026. On April 2nd, I will start carefully reintroducing things to my life and seeing how they affect me.

I am recording each day of the declutter to see how eliminating the ambient digital distractions from my life improves. I've occasionally seen remarkable improvement with two-day long "digital fasts" but have lacked the discipline to enforce them for a longer period of time. I frequently slip-up and lose interest in bearing out the first few days of boredom. In order to combat that this time, I am making a $20:$0 bet with a friend on whether I can ride out the month (e.g. if I slip-up, I pay $20, but if I don't, they don't pay anything).

Restricted Activities and My Substitutes

During a digital declutter, Cal Newport advises creating a list of restricted activities and pairing that with a list of alternatives to intentionally use instead. In full honesty, I have trouble revisiting Cal Newport's work because I dislike his writing style and find his recent writing on AI to be borderline sophistic and occasionally even incorrect, but his ideas on technology use and the value of uninterrupted focus are often good and original regardless. Sometimes detailed elaborations on common sense ideas, like much of Newport's work is, can be tremendously valuable to aiding implementation. I owe many of my accomplishments to carefully following Newport's ideas.

The following is a list of things I'm going to be cutting out or severely restricting:

  • YouTube
  • Reddit
  • Twitter
  • Discord (except for 15 minutes on Sunday and Wednesday to check information for IRL meetups I attend)
  • Podcasts
  • Music
  • Online meetups
  • My phone
  • Hacker News, Substack, Forum browsing, "scrolling news"
  • Caffeine (added March 3rd)
  • No TV (unless with friends)
  • Text messaging: limit texting to scheduling calls (I don't communicate much over text message)

Since I sometimes need YouTube and Reddit when searching for things, I am only barring them as recreational activities. Using them to fill silence and using them intentionally to find information are completely separate in my experience. I am blocking reddit on all devices and only accessing it when necessary via the internet archive.

I have decided not to limit my use of LLMs, though they are a new technology, since I don't use them for entertainment or recreation and mostly use them as tutor or like Google to help me understand things.

Since I need my phone for maps and communication sometimes, I'm going to leave it at home in its lockbox for a month, using a physical keyboard connected via USB to type if necessary. I am switching to an old flip phone I will carry around at school instead to take calls in urgent situations. (Update March 5th: This has proved difficult over the last week for scheduling reasons but I will implement this starting Saturday).

Broadly, I aim to eliminate the internet as a space for recreation or ambiently filling silent or bored moments, which means even cutting out activities like aimlessly browsing Wikipedia or Googling things for fun, though it would be too much to cut out Wikipedia or Google as services.

The following is a list of things I'm going to substitute them with:

  • Staring at a wall and going for walks in place of using the internet as a distraction to quell my negative emotions
  • Reading books in place of the internet to fill silent moments or Googling things for fun
  • Homework in place of obsessively checking the news to keep track of things
  • My different fitness hobbies, e.g. climbing, lifting, running in place of aimless online recreation
  • Applying for jobs in place of chatting on discord
  • Clubs (climbing, philosophy club) in place of socializing on discord
  • Meeting friends in place of socializing on discord or twitter
  • Flip phone in place of phone
  • Herbal tea instead of coffee

Daily Log

March 2nd

5:15PM – Wrote my list of restrictions, told online friends I would be offline for accountability, logged out of all social media accounts, and made the $20 enforcement agreement with friend. My month offline has begun.

I still feel a little bit like social media is "accessible" through the internet because I haven't added youtube to my website blocker. I want to feel offline in a way I'm not sure this rule allows. I will see how I feel in a bit. Having so much access to the internet feels like leaving a million tabs open in my brain, and creating new restrictions is like force quitting the whole application. It might take some time to fully close those loops. Aside from that small gap in my rule set, I feel quite confident about this project overall given the harshness of the agreement I made and severity for failure.

March 3rd

10:44AM

Yesterday I went to bed at 10:30. It was a little later than I liked but I managed to read a book for 20 minutes prior to sleeping. Apparently Bryan Johnson is right about this, because my HRV was higher and resting heart rate was lower than usual. My resting heart rate is around 60BPM. In the summer my average was around 55BPM until I over-trained one week and it's since then hovered at 60BPM. I'm not sure what to do to lower it again. Maybe my cardio is undertrained? Maybe I'm just suffering a residual overtraining that hasn't abated despite deload weeks? I'm not sure. I'll start with trying to do 20 minutes of cardio every day with two of those sessions being HIIT.

Not having the internet as a potential distraction to whittle away my morning on meant I made it to the 9:45 bus when I usually procrastinate for a few hours and take the 10:39 bus to school, all despite waking up slightly late today. I did read a book before leaving for the bus too, meaning I could've taken an earlier bus but I stopped early enough to leave on time so I don't think it's worth changing the rules to combat this behavior. I think I need about 9 hours of sleep a night given how intense my lifting + climbing is right now. I find deadlifting massively increases fatigue and I often need days to feel recovered.

I read some of Nick Bostrom's old book Superintelligence on the bus to school. I find it's much easier to read without the potential for a distraction, since it feels like the possibility of opening twitter always tugs on my attention. I also didn't listen to any music on the bus except for half a minute when I was trying to find a sound to play on my headphones to block out the rattling of the bus. I don't consider that a slip-up from the policy or using music for recreation though. Interestingly, that half a minute sounded really good. I'm reminded of the time that Michael Pollan (who might be a fraud or dumbass but that's irrelevant to this) said he quit coffee for a month and his first cup of coffee after his abstinence felt noticeably wonderful. The same might be true of music, if you stop listening to it constantly to fill silence. I don't think that the brain adapts to music the way it adapts to coffee. Taking caffeine every day would lead to adaptation fast, but listening to music daily probably wouldn't. The key is to have the sound feel novel. So maybe a practice like allowing most of your day to be silent, would neutralize the adaption – whereas you might have to drink coffee once a week or less to not adapt to it.

I don't benefit from most stimulants I take and dislike the psychological effect of almost all stimulants – caffeine being the only exception. Being adapted to caffeine, like I am now, has no benefit. I've decided to quit caffeine for this declutter month, and take it like once a week after that for recreation.

Along with music, catching the bus on time, and reading feeling easy – another instant change I've noticed from the declutter is that I eat so much faster. I usually wait until like 8pm to start eating protein to fill out my macros, but yesterday I came home and instantly made a protein shake and ate my ration of greek yogurt. That was quite surprising and a nice change. I've read from other accounts of declutters that people get similar nice behavioral changes that show up on week 2 as well. When not constantly distracted, it seems I need so much less will power to move through life. Despite what I thought, I have the executive function of an average 20-year old (or better), but it seems being distracted all the time makes me functionally a 10-year old.

Regarding work itself, though my situation with sleep and overall distractedness is improving, I did just spend like 25 minutes writing this instead of working – so I don't want keeping this log to be a distraction. I might have to come up with a system to prevent that.

12:45PM

Made it to an office hours meeting on time and finished one assignment ahead of deadline – both of which are quite rare for me. I still feel the "tug" of the internet on my attention, like the tabs being open in my mind that I mentioned yesterday. It's annoying. Either this will go away in a few days as my mind adapts, or I might have to make some other change. I'm not sure what it is.

4:44PM

I went for a run/walk outside. Like food, I also usually procrastinate on this sort of thing or drag my feet for hours, but today after coming home, I ate lunch and then went out with little delay. I didn't bring my phone. It was different from other times I've been in a more distracted state and gone out without my phone, since this time I felt less of the agonizing adaptation to a less stimulated environment. I think that's evidence in favor of the "tab closing" taking a few days. I continue to be impressed how fast my executive function has bounced back, and how will power is often not needed, and extremely effective when applied. I'm really hoping this continues.

I'm also adding a rule that I can either work on productive tasks or do nothing between 9AM and 6PM (with exceptions for clubs and 30 minutes for food). This would help since I was slightly distracted by reading today. I did like going for a walk though, but I could have used it to catch up on work. The "work/do nothing" only applies unless I finish all my work before 6PM. I think Tim Urban mentioned a system of having an 8 hour period where you try to get four hours of work done as fast as possible, and four hours "off". The goal is to get the four hours out of the way as fast as possible, so the last four hours would be as nice as possible. I might consider something similar later.

March 4th

12:14PM

I made it to school on time today, despite waking up later than intended and having awful sleep. Not having the option to access my phone in the morning meant I drove to the bus-stop early. This is the second time I've shown up to school on time in the entire semester, so that's good. This is a pretty incredible change with a direct cause, which is extremely nice.

After my morning classes I wasn't as productive as I wanted to be, but I did eat lunch and only really "lost" an hour between 11PM and now.

I feel intense guilt for any time that gets underutilized when I've intentionally restricted myself from going to the gym this week. I've tried to commit to not missing any workouts but circumstances make that dumb to prioritize over other stuff. Still, I spent an hour outside yesterday (which was good) and then came home and got little done for a reason I'm not quite sure of. I could have gone to the gym instead.

The upside is that even if I use this time imperfectly, just getting done with everything means that next week I'll have much more time and be much less distracted. In the past when I've done mini-declutters, I don't procrastinate on going to the gym. So when I allow myself to go to the gym again, I'm confident I'll get to the level of discipline in my fitness and climbing I feel I've been falling short of. I like Jocko Willink's philosophy on discipline but prior to the declutter felt I couldn't really click with it. If the effectiveness of your will power is fundamentally bottlenecked or restricted (as constant distraction does to it), then mentally forcing yourself to do something just doesn't work. It's like pressing the accelerator in a car that won't start. The point of discipline is to consciously push that accelerator – not to constantly get out of the car and be forced to manually push it, which would break anyone's resolve – but something about my exposure to the internet reliably disrupts the engine to the point I've had to shove the car everywhere I want it to go my whole life.

I think my ADHD-tendencies make me more vulnerable to harm from distractions. If you gave my psychology and low resistance to distraction to the average person, it would ruin their life. To everyone else, the ADHD makes me look like I'm just "lazy". The irony is if you swapped our positions I think I would just vastly exceed most people and most people would fair far worse than I do even when the ADHD is least controllable. At the risk of arrogance, I think what I'll get done over the next few weeks – and even after that – should do well to prove my point. Well, I guess it's just arrogance. I'm extremely angry at my years of constant failure and personal dissapointment with myself and I'm very much "out for blood" to prove my capability. I think that will be useful. In some respects I have a deeply violent undercurrent to my personality. I just don't accept not getting what I want. I think I see aspects of myself in the There Will Be Blood guy or Alex Honnold. The overlapping portions aren't the good or effective parts. It's a very dark part of myself. Most who share these aspects probably end up as heroine addicts and not successful entrepenuers or climbers. (Honnold's partial auto-biography "Alone on the Wall" paints a pretty different picture of him than the media coverage does and which Free Solo only lightly touches on, and it's what I'm referring to here.) But regardless, when used correctly this ego-centric desire to prove you're better than everyone can be effective fuel. It's just that effective fuel can drive you into a ditch just as fast as it can take you somewhere worth going. These traits are probably hereditary. It's important to have a stopping point to it, like Honnold appeared to have with El Cap and which the There Will Be Blood guy just entirely lacked in the movie. I think the only way to get this to stop is to have some kind of goal like Honnold did. I think it stems from some kind of deeply-ingrained feeling of inadequacy and self-resentment that starts as a child and just becomes core to your personality as you get older. All you have to do to get it to stop is free solo El Cap I guess.

The goal of a digital declutter, however, is not simply to enjoy time away from intrusive technology. This period should be one of strenuous activity and experimentation.

You want to arrive at the end of the declutter having rediscovered the type of activities that generate real satisfaction, enabling you to confidently craft a better life—one in which technology serves only a supporting role for more meaningful ends.

In terms of activities to "aggressively explore", I'm thinking of segmenting this into two categories: Deep Solo Activities and Deep Social Activities. Deep Solo Activities are things I do primarily alone and that serve some value or interest in a serious way instead of a shallow, low-value way. This means reading papers instead of scrolling twitter, reading a book instead of a news article, learning something deeply instead of just saving a YouTube video on it to a playlist to come back to later. Deep Social Activities are the same but involve things done in-person with other people.

I will come up with a list of these later and update Restricted Activities and My Substitutes section. This will also be a good reason to extend the declutter by a few weeks.

I notice I'm still very resistant to some work. I think I really just don't want to do it. I find lab sections of my classes fun and easy – even if the work is hard, I just learn so fast in that environment – and still cannot for the life of me sit down to study. I love time pressure and the competitive aspect of labs. I've noticed this to be true on and off medication. I think this is due to the ADHD and not really solvable through cutting out distractions.
